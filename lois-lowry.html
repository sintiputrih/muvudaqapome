Lois Lowry Biography

Lois Lowry is an American writer and author who was born and brought up in Honolulu, Hawaii, United States of America. She is widely known for authoring several books for children and young adults, including Number the Stars and The Giver Quartet. Lowry is also recognized for writing about difficult subject matters and complex themes in works for young audiences. In 1990, she won two Newbery Medals: for Number the Stars and The Giver 1994. Her book Gooney Bird Greene won the 2002 Rhode Island Children’s Book Award.

One of her most well-known works is “The Giver,” published in 1993. It is a dystopian novel set in a society where emotions and memories are suppressed. “The Giver” won the prestigious Newbery Medal in 1994, recognizing it as the most distinguished contribution to American literature for children.

Lowry has also written three companion novels to “The Giver” known as “The Giver Quartet,” which includes “Gathering Blue,” “Messenger,” and “Son.” These books expand on the themes and concepts introduced in the original novel.

Another notable work by Lowry is “Number the Stars,” published in 1989, which tells the story of a young Danish girl named Annemarie during World War II. The book received critical acclaim and won the Newbery Medal in 1990.

Throughout her career, Lois Lowry has received numerous awards and honors for her contributions to children’s literature. Her books often explore themes of individuality, freedom, and social issues, resonating with readers of various ages.

    Early Life and Education

    Lois Lowry was born Lois Ann Hammersberg on March 20, 1937, in Honolulu, Hawaii. She was the middle child of three siblings. Her father, Robert E. Hammersberg, was an Army dentist, and her mother, Katharine Gordon Landis, was a teacher.

    Due to her father’s military service, Lowry’s family moved frequently during her childhood, living in different parts of the United States and abroad. She spent her early years in places like Pennsylvania, Tokyo, and New York City. The frequent moves exposed her to various cultures and experiences, which would later influence her writing.

    Lowry developed a love for reading and writing from an early age. She was an avid reader and often escaped into books as a way to cope with the challenges of moving and adjusting to new environments. Her passion for storytelling grew, and she began writing her own stories and poems.

    After completing high school, Lowry attended Brown University in Providence, Rhode Island, where she studied literature and writing. She graduated with a Bachelor of Arts degree in 1958.

    Following her graduation, Lowry pursued a career in writing and worked as a journalist and photographer for various publications. She also got married and had four children. While raising her family, she continued to write, primarily focusing on children’s literature.

    Lois Lowry’s early life experiences, including her exposure to different cultures and her love for reading, played a significant role in shaping her writing career. Her varied background and personal experiences have infused her stories with a rich tapestry of themes and perspectives.

    Rise to Fame

    Lois Lowry’s rise to fame as an author began with the publication of her first novel, “A Summer to Die,” in 1977. The book was inspired by the death of her own sister and tells the story of a young girl coping with the loss of her sister to leukemia. Although it received positive reviews, it didn’t gain widespread attention.

    Lowry’s breakthrough came with the publication of “Number the Stars” in 1989. The book, set during World War II, tells the story of a young Danish girl helping her Jewish friend escape from the Nazis. “Number the Stars” received critical acclaim and won the Newbery Medal in 1990, catapulting Lowry to prominence in the world of children’s literature.

    However, it was her novel “The Giver,” published in 1993, that firmly established Lois Lowry as a prominent author. “The Giver” received widespread acclaim and became a bestseller. The book explores themes of individuality, freedom, and the consequences of a society that suppresses emotions and memories. It won the prestigious Newbery Medal in 1994 and has since become a staple in classrooms and a beloved classic among readers of all ages.

    “The Giver” and its subsequent companion novels, collectively known as “The Giver Quartet,” further solidified Lowry’s reputation as a master storyteller. The quartet includes “Gathering Blue” (2000), “Messenger” (2004), and “Son” (2012), each expanding upon the world and themes introduced in the original book.

    Throughout her career, Lowry has continued to write thought-provoking and impactful books for young readers. Her works often tackle complex subjects and encourage readers to question societal norms and explore the power of individuality and empathy.

    Lois Lowry The Giver

    The Giver Quartet is a series of four books about a dystopian world by Lois Lowry. The quartet consists of The Giver (1993), Gathering Blue (2000), Messenger (2004), and Son (2012). The first book won the 1994 Newbery Medal and has sold more than 10 million copies. The story takes place in the world of the Giver. Each book has a different protagonist but is set in the same futuristic era.

    Lois Lowry Number The Stars

    Number the Stars is a work of historical fiction by the American author Lois Lowry about the escape of a family of Jews from Copenhagen, Denmark, during World War II. The story centers on 10-year-old Annemarie Johansen, who lives with her mother, father, and sister Kirsti in Copenhagen in 1943.

    Annemarie becomes a part of the events related to the rescue of the Danish Jews when thousands of Jews were to reach the neutral ground in Sweden to avoid being relocated to concentration camps. She risks her life to help her best friend, Ellen Rosen, by pretending that Ellen is Annemarie’s late older sister, Lise, who was killed earlier in the war by the Nazi army because of her work with the Danish Resistance.
